# Sector Expert Methodology

## Scope

The sector expert evaluates market structure, customer needs, product adoption, competition, technology routes, supply chains, and defensibility. It does not certify issuer accounts or legal compliance. Industry background is a comparison framework, not proof of issuer performance.

Use `EVID-*` for company, product, customer, supplier, and market claims tied to the deal. Use private `KBREF-*` sources for market-sizing methods, taxonomy, benchmark questions, and technical challenge patterns. Label any transfer from a generic benchmark as an assumption until issuer Evidence supports it.

## Market Sizing

Define the unit, geography, customer segment, price basis, and forecast period before presenting TAM, SAM, or SOM. Prefer bottom-up calculations using customer counts, deployment rates, capacity, price, or usage. Reconcile bottom-up results with credible top-down sources and explain differences. Avoid treating TAM as obtainable revenue.

Connect SOM to sales capacity, qualification cycles, production constraints, channel access, regulation, and competition. State formulae, units, dates, and uncertainty ranges.

## Competition And Technology

Map direct competitors, substitutes, customer in-house alternatives, and emerging routes. Compare on requirements that determine purchase decisions rather than generic feature lists. Test whether differentiation is measurable, durable, and economically valuable.

For technical claims, distinguish demonstrated performance, certified performance, production-scale performance, and roadmap targets. Examine dependency on scarce inputs, suppliers, manufacturing yield, integration partners, standards, and customer validation. A patent count alone is not a moat.

## Commercial Validation

Assess customer concentration, pipeline quality, conversion stages, renewal or repeat purchase, pricing power, switching costs, and referenceability. Separate signed contracts, non-binding intentions, pilots, and management forecasts. Look for survivorship bias in customer examples.

The report should state market facts, issuer facts, derived calculations, assumptions, counter-evidence, and missing diligence. R2 explains changed conclusions. R3 addresses a specific market or technology dispute with direct evidence and falsifiable tests.
